## The fee nobody notices

If you ask a mutual fund investor what their expense ratio is, most will stare at you blankly. The expense ratio — the annual fee a fund charges for managing your money — is deducted from the NAV daily. It never appears as a line item in your statement. You never write a cheque for it. This invisibility is precisely why it matters so much.

## SEBI's expense ratio framework

SEBI last revised the expense ratio framework in 2018, introducing a sliding scale based on AUM:

| AUM Slab (₹ crore) | Maximum TER (Equity) |
|---|---|
| 0 - 500 | 2.25% |
| 500 - 750 | 2.00% |
| 750 - 2,000 | 1.75% |
| 2,000 - 5,000 | 1.50% |
| 5,000 - 10,000 | 1.35% |
| 10,000 - 50,000 | 1.15% |
| Above 50,000 | 1.05% |

Plus a 0.20% allowance for closing distribution expenses and an additional 0.05% for new inflows from tier-2 and tier-3 cities.

Before this framework, many large equity funds charged 2.25-2.50% regardless of AUM. The regulation forced economies of scale to pass through to investors.

## What you actually save

Take India's largest equity fund by AUM — roughly ₹70,000 crore at current levels. Pre-2018, it charged about 2.25%. Post-2018, it charges about 1.05% (plus the distribution allowance). The difference is roughly 1%.

On ₹70,000 crore AUM, 1% is ₹700 crore per year. That is the annual saving for investors in that single fund. Across all equity mutual fund AUM of roughly ₹25 lakh crore, SEBI estimates the aggregate annual saving from expense ratio rationalisation at approximately ₹3,500-4,000 crore.

For an individual with a ₹50,000 monthly SIP over 20 years:

- **At 2.25% expense ratio**: Final corpus approximately ₹68 lakh
- **At 1.05% expense ratio**: Final corpus approximately ₹82 lakh

The 1.2% difference in fees produces a ₹14 lakh difference in outcome. Compounding does not just work on your returns. It works on your costs.

## What SEBI has not done

Two significant cost components remain outside the expense ratio cap:

**Transaction costs and brokerage**: Funds incur brokerage when buying and selling securities. These are not included in the TER. For actively traded funds, they can add 0.10-0.20% effectively.

**GST on management fees**: The 18% GST on fund management fees is borne by the scheme and adds roughly 0.15-0.20% to the effective cost.

After both, the effective cost of that "1.05% TER" fund is closer to 1.30-1.45%. Still far better than 2.25%, but not quite as lean as the headline suggests.

## The action you should take

Check your fund's expense ratio on the AMFI website. If you are in a regular plan, the TER is typically 0.6-1% higher than the direct plan. Switching from regular to direct is the single highest-impact, lowest-effort financial decision available to most Indian investors. It requires no market timing, no research, and no additional risk.

## The bottom line

SEBI's expense ratio caps have saved investors real money — thousands of crores annually. But the biggest remaining inefficiency is not between funds. It is between the regular plan and the direct plan of the same fund. Fix that one thing, and you have captured most of the benefit.
