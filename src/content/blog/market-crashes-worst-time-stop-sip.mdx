## The instinct that costs you the most

In March 2020, the NIFTY 50 fell 38% in five weeks. SIP stoppage ratios — the percentage of SIPs discontinued versus new ones started — spiked to over 50% according to AMFI data. People did what felt sensible: they stopped putting money into something that was falling.

This is the single most expensive behavioural error in retail investing. Let us walk through why.

## What happens when you pause

Assume a ₹20,000 monthly SIP in a NIFTY-tracking fund starting January 2018. By February 2020, your corpus is roughly ₹6.1 lakh. Then COVID hits. The market falls 38%. Your corpus drops to roughly ₹3.8 lakh. You panic and pause your SIP from March 2020 through December 2020.

By December 2021, the NIFTY had recovered and then some. If you had continued your SIP through the crash:

- **Total invested**: ₹9.6 lakh (48 months x ₹20,000)
- **Corpus value by Dec 2021**: roughly ₹14.2 lakh

If you paused for 10 months:

- **Total invested**: ₹7.6 lakh (38 months x ₹20,000)
- **Corpus value by Dec 2021**: roughly ₹10.1 lakh

You saved ₹2 lakh in contributions. You gave up roughly ₹4.1 lakh in corpus value. The ₹2 lakh you did not invest during the crash would have bought units at steeply discounted NAVs — the cheapest purchases in years. You skipped the sale.

## The mathematics of recovery

Here is the structural reason crashes are the worst time to stop. A 38% fall requires a 61% rise to recover. But your SIP buys more units when prices are low. During the March-August 2020 period, a ₹20,000 SIP was purchasing roughly 60% more units per month than it had in January 2020. When the market recovered, those extra units amplified your gains disproportionately.

Rupee-cost averaging is not just a comforting phrase. It is a mathematical mechanism that automatically loads you up on cheap assets. Stopping your SIP disables the mechanism precisely when it is most useful.

## The SWP corollary: when crashes actually hurt

There is a scenario where crashes genuinely damage you: if you are in the withdrawal phase. A systematic withdrawal plan (SWP) during a crash is the mirror image of a paused SIP — you are selling cheap units, permanently depleting your corpus.

If you are drawing ₹30,000 monthly from a ₹60 lakh equity corpus and a 40% crash hits in year 2, your corpus may not survive 20 years. The sequence-of-returns risk is devastating for withdrawers and beneficial for accumulators.

## The honest prescription

If you are still accumulating wealth — and most people under 50 are — a market crash is not a reason to stop investing. It is a reason to continue, or even increase, your SIP. Your future self will thank you for the cheap units you bought when everyone else was fleeing.

If you are withdrawing wealth, a crash is exactly when you need a contingency plan. Keep 2-3 years of withdrawals in liquid or short-duration debt funds so you are not forced to sell equities at depressed prices.

The market does not care about your feelings. But your investment behaviour should not be determined by your feelings either.
