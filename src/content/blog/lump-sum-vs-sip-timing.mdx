## The question everyone asks wrong

"Should I invest this ₹5 lakh as a lump sum or through a SIP?"

This question, as posed, is unanswerable. The correct answer depends on a variable most people omit: when in the market cycle are you asking?

## What the data says

Vanguard's well-known study across multiple markets shows that lump sum investing beats dollar-cost averaging (the academic term for SIP) approximately 68% of the time over 10-year periods. Indian data from Nifty 50 confirms this pattern. The reason is simple: markets go up more often than they go down. By spreading your investment over months, you keep money in low-return cash for longer, missing early gains.

But that 68% conceals the severity of the 32%. When lump sum loses, it loses badly. Invest ₹5 lakh in January 2008, and by March 2009 your portfolio was worth approximately ₹1.8 lakh. The same ₹5 lakh invested via SIP over 24 months from January 2008 would have been worth approximately ₹5.6 lakh by January 2010. Same market, opposite emotional experience.

## The real question

The question is not which method is statistically superior. The question is: what will you do if your lump sum drops 30% in the first six months?

- If your honest answer is "I will hold and wait," lump sum is likely better for you.
- If your honest answer is "I will panic, sell, and blame the market," SIP is the only method that will keep you invested.

Most people believe they are in the first category. Experience shows most people are in the second.

## A practical framework

For a windfall — bonus, inheritance, property sale — use this approach:

1. **₹0-3 lakh**: Lump sum. The absolute risk is small. Market timing anxiety over ₹3 lakh is not worth the opportunity cost of staying in cash.

2. **₹3-10 lakh**: SIP over 6-12 months. You capture most of the statistical advantage of early investment while reducing the regret of a badly timed entry by roughly 40%.

3. **₹10 lakh+**: SIP over 12-18 months. The behavioral risk increases with amount. A ₹15 lakh lump sum that drops 25% becomes a ₹11.25 lakh portfolio and a sleepless investor. A 12-month SIP into the same market smooths the entry and, more importantly, the psychology.

## The inconvenient truth

Neither method compensates for inadequate savings. A ₹5,000/month SIP and a ₹60,000 annual lump sum are the same amount of money. The debate over delivery mechanism is secondary to the debate over quantity. Save more. Invest consistently. The method matters less than the discipline.
