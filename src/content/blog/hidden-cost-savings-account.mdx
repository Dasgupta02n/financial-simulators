## The money that stays put

The average Indian urban household keeps ₹2-5 lakh in savings accounts. It is accessible, it feels safe, and the bank occasionally sends a polite message about how your balance is growing. The problem is that it is not growing. It is shrinking.

## The silent subtraction

SBI and most major banks offer 2.7-3.5% on savings accounts. HDFC, ICICI, and Axis offer similar rates — typically 3% for balances below ₹1 crore. This is not a return. This is a token gesture.

Now apply the two adjustments that matter:

- **Tax** — savings account interest is taxable as "Income from Other Sources." Under Section 80TTA, only ₹10,000 of interest is deductible (₹50,000 for senior citizens under 80TTB). For a ₹5 lakh balance at 3%, you earn ₹15,000 in interest. In the 20% tax bracket, you pay ₹3,000 tax. Net return: 2.4%.

- **Inflation** — at 5.5% CPI inflation, the real return is 2.4% minus 5.5% = **-3.1%**.

Your ₹5 lakh in a savings account loses approximately ₹15,500 in purchasing power every year. You are paying the bank for the privilege of declining wealth.

## The three-bucket system

Not all money in a savings account should be moved. Some of it serves a purpose — immediate liquidity. The solution is to sort your idle cash into three buckets:

**Bucket 1: Operating balance (keep in savings account)**
1-2 months of expenses. This is money you need within 48 hours — rent, groceries, school fees, EMIs. Accept the negative real return as the cost of instant access.

**Bucket 2: Short-term reserve (move to FD or liquid fund)**
3-9 months of expenses beyond your operating balance. A 6-month FD at 6.5-7% or a liquid mutual fund at approximately 5.5-6% post-expense ratio. You can break the FD or redeem the liquid fund in 1-2 business days. The difference versus a savings account on ₹3 lakh over a year: approximately ₹6,000-10,000 in additional interest. Small, but real.

**Bucket 3: Long-term surplus (move to equity or debt funds)**
Any money you will not need for 3+ years. This should not be in a savings account, an FD, or anywhere near one. Equity mutual funds, PPF, NPS — these are instruments designed to beat inflation. A savings account is not.

## The auto-sweep option

Most banks offer an auto-sweep facility where balances above a threshold automatically convert to short-term FDs. This raises your effective rate from 3% to approximately 6-6.5% with zero effort. Enable it. It is the only financial product in India that makes you more money by doing literally nothing differently.

## The bottom line

A savings account is a transaction tool, not an investment. Using it as the primary store of wealth is the most common and most expensive mistake in Indian personal finance.
