₹5 crore. Say it aloud. It sounds like a number that belongs on a yacht, not in a middle-class retirement spreadsheet. Yet this is the figure most Indian professionals cite when asked about their retirement target. It is also, almost without exception, wrong.

The problem is not that ₹5 crore is small. The problem is that ₹5 crore in 2026 is not ₹5 crore in 2046, and the difference is not a rounding error — it is a catastrophe disguised as arithmetic.

## The math of erosion

At 6% inflation — India's long-term average, not the politically convenient recent lows — ₹5 crore today has the purchasing power of roughly ₹1.56 crore in 20 years. That is not a prediction. That is a certainty embedded in the definition of inflation. You do not need a market forecast; you need a calculator and the willingness to face its output.

Now consider spending. A modest middle-class household in a Tier-1 Indian city spends about ₹50,000 per month today. Adjust that for 6% annual inflation over 20 years, and you arrive at roughly ₹1.6 lakh per month. Annual expenditure: ₹19.2 lakh. Divide ₹5 crore by ₹19.2 lakh. You get approximately 26 years. That sounds reasonable — until you remember that expenses do not stay flat. Medical costs rise at 12-15% annually. A single hospitalization in year 18 of retirement can consume what you carefully budgeted for groceries in year 19.

## The three hidden devourers

**Inflation** we have discussed. It is the obvious one, and people still underestimate it.

**Longevity risk** is the second. An Indian male retiring at 60 has a life expectancy of approximately 77, but the *conditional* life expectancy at 60 — meaning if you have already reached 60, which you have — is roughly 19 more years, and the 90th percentile lives to 88. Your corpus must survive you, not the average person. Averages are for statisticians; your bank account deals in specifics.

**Sequence-of-returns risk** is the third and most insidious. If the market crashes in the first three years of your retirement, your corpus never recovers — even if the market subsequently rallies. You are withdrawing while the portfolio is depleted, permanently reducing the base that would benefit from the recovery. This is the financial equivalent of getting pneumonia upon arrival at a hill station: the vacation never quite recovers.

## The real number

For a 25-year retirement in India with a ₹50,000/month lifestyle (today's terms), a safe withdrawal rate closer to 3.2% (not the American 4%), and a corpus that must weather at least one major market crash — the required number is closer to ₹7-8 crore. Possibly more if you have dependents or chronic health conditions.

This is not a pleasant conclusion. Pleasant conclusions are the province of mutual fund advertisements, which feature smiling retirees on beaches — presumably because the alternative, retirees staring anxiously at inflation charts, does not move product.

## What to do

First, calculate your *actual* number, not the aspirational one. Use the FIRE Matrix tool above with your real expenses — not the expenses you think you will have, but the ones your credit card statement confirms you already do.

Second, account for medical inflation separately. It will eat your corpus faster than any other line item.

Third, plan for 30 years of retirement, not 20. Indian life expectancy is rising by roughly 2.5 years per decade. By the time you retire, 90 will be the new 80.

₹5 crore is a milestone, not a destination. The difference matters more than most people will admit — until they are 73, withdrawing from a shrinking corpus, and wondering where the yacht went.
