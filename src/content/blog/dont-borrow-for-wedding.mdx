## The most expensive day of your life

The average Indian wedding costs between ₹10 lakh and ₹25 lakh depending on the city and community. A significant portion of this — often 30-40% — is financed through personal loans, credit cards, or gold loans. The wedding industry in India, valued at approximately $50 billion, has a vested interest in making you believe that spending less means loving less.

Let us examine what borrowing for a wedding actually costs.

## The math of a ₹15 lakh wedding loan

- **Loan amount** — ₹15 lakh
- **Interest rate** — 11% (typical personal loan rate in 2026)
- **Tenure** — 5 years
- **EMI** — ₹32,600/month
- **Total repayment** — ₹19.56 lakh
- **Interest paid** — ₹4.56 lakh

You borrowed ₹15 lakh for a single day. You will spend 60 months paying back ₹19.56 lakh. The interest alone — ₹4.56 lakh — could have been a year's SIP of ₹38,000/month, growing to approximately ₹7.8 lakh in 5 years at 12% equity returns.

## The cascade effect

A wedding loan does not exist in isolation. It creates a cascade:

1. **EMI reduces investable income** — ₹32,600/month going to loan repayment means ₹32,600 not going to SIPs, emergency funds, or retirement savings. Over 5 years, that is ₹19.5 lakh of cash flow that produced nothing except a memory and a debt.

2. **It delays home buying** — banks calculate your total EMI obligations. A ₹32,600 EMI reduces your home loan eligibility by approximately ₹18-20 lakh. The flat you wanted to buy at 30 is now the flat you buy at 35, after five more years of rent.

3. **It compounds in reverse** — the ₹15 lakh you didn't invest at age 28 would have become approximately ₹1.4 crore by age 55 at 12% annual returns. You traded ₹1.4 crore of future wealth for a mandap and a catering bill.

## What a ₹6 lakh wedding looks like

A ₹6 lakh wedding — venue at a simple banquet hall, catering for 150 guests, modest decor, no loan — costs exactly what it costs. No EMI. No interest. No cascade. The ₹9 lakh difference (between a ₹6 lakh cash wedding and a ₹15 lakh borrowed one) invested as a SIP of ₹15,000/month for 5 years at 12% grows to approximately ₹12.3 lakh.

You can then use that ₹12.3 lakh as a down payment on a house, a child's education fund, or simply the beginning of a financial life that doesn't start in a hole.

## The cultural pressure

The argument against lavish weddings in India is often met with "but what will people say." This is not a financial argument. It is a social one. And it is valid — social pressure in Indian families is real and heavy. But the people who attend your wedding will not help you repay the loan. The caterer will not accept your EMI schedule. The decorator is already booked for next weekend.

A wedding is a celebration. A loan is a contract. Conflating the two is the most expensive confusion in Indian personal finance.
