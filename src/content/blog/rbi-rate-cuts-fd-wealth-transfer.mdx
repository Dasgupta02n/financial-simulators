## The quiet tax on savers

Between February 2025 and April 2026, the RBI cut the repo rate by a cumulative 150 basis points — from 6.50% to 5.00%. This was cheered by borrowers and barely noticed by the people who actually fund India's savings: fixed deposit holders.

Here is what happened. SBI's 1-year FD rate dropped from roughly 7.10% to around 6.40%. HDFC's went from 7.20% to 6.50%. Smaller banks followed. If you had ₹10 lakh in an FD renewing during this period, your annual interest income fell by roughly ₹7,000 — before tax.

## The mathematics of the transfer

This is not an accident. It is the explicit mechanism of monetary policy. When RBI cuts the repo rate, it wants banks to lend more cheaply so businesses borrow and invest. Banks fund loans from deposits. If deposit rates stay high while lending rates fall, the bank's margin compresses. So banks cut deposit rates. Your FD income goes down. The borrower's EMI goes down. You subsidised the borrower. You were not consulted.

Over five years of rolling FD renewals in a declining rate environment, the cumulative loss on a ₹10 lakh FD compared to the pre-cut rate is approximately ₹35,000-40,000 in interest.

## Then inflation finishes the job

RBI typically cuts rates when growth is slowing, which sometimes coincides with inflation remaining sticky. India's CPI inflation averaged 4.8% through 2025-26. A 6.4% FD minus 4.8% inflation leaves a real return of 1.6%. After tax at your slab rate (say 30%), your post-tax real return is roughly 0.8%.

That is not a savings instrument. That is a waiting room where your money sits patiently losing purchasing power.

## What can you do?

Short of predicting RBI's next move — which professional economists routinely fail at — you can:

- **Ladder your FDs** across 1, 2, and 3-year tenures. When rates fall, only the maturing portion reinvests at lower rates.
- **Consider a partial allocation to debt mutual funds**, where the mark-to-market mechanism at least lets you benefit when rates eventually rise again.
- **Do not lock in for 5+ years** in a falling rate environment. The penalty for premature withdrawal is usually lower than the opportunity cost of being stuck at a low rate.

## The honest bottom line

FDs are safe in nominal terms. In real terms, they have been slowly losing ground for a decade. RBI's rate cuts accelerate this. Understand the mechanism, plan accordingly, and stop treating FDs as a growth instrument. They are capital preservation tools, and right now, that preservation is barely keeping pace.
