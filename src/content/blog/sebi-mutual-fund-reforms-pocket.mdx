## Reform as spectator sport

Every few years, SEBI announces mutual fund reforms. Headlines declare investors will save thousands of crores. Then life resumes, and most people cannot tell you what changed. Let us fix that.

## The big reforms, briefly

**2018: Entry load ban (reiterated).** SEBI had banned entry loads way back in 2009, but by 2018 the industry had found workarounds through advisory fees and distribution commissions baked into expense ratios. SEBI tightened this. The direct saving: if you were paying a 1% entry load on a ₹5 lakh lump sum, that is ₹5,000 you now keep.

**2018: Expense ratio cuts for large AUM funds.** SEBI created a sliding scale — the larger a fund's AUM, the lower its permissible expense ratio. For funds above ₹50,000 crore AUM, the maximum fell from 2.25% to roughly 1.05% for equity funds. On a ₹10 lakh investment, that is a saving of about ₹1,200 per year.

**2020: Categorisation and rationalisation.** SEBI forced funds into clearly defined categories. No more "multi-cap" funds that were actually large-cap, or "balanced" funds that held 70% equity. This did not save money directly, but it stopped you from buying the wrong product disguised as the right one. The financial equivalent of truth-in-labelling.

**2023: Small AUM fund fee increase.** In a rare reversal, SEBI allowed funds with AUM below ₹500 crore to charge slightly higher expense ratios to remain viable. A small fund that cannot cover its compliance costs simply closes — and then you have no small-cap option at all. This was pragmatic, not generous.

## What it means for you, in rupees

Consider a ₹25,000 monthly SIP over 10 years in a large-cap fund:

- **Pre-2018 expense ratio (2.25%)**: Your corpus would be roughly ₹55.6 lakh
- **Post-2018 expense ratio (~1.05%)**: Your corpus would be roughly ₹61.8 lakh

That is approximately ₹6.2 lakh more in your account — not from higher returns, but from lower fees. Compounding works on costs just as ruthlessly as it works on returns.

## What SEBI has not fixed (yet)

Distribution commissions remain embedded in regular plans. The direct vs regular plan gap persists — typically 0.6-1% annually. Over 20 years, this single gap can cost you more than all the reforms combined have saved you. Switch to direct plans if you can select funds without advice. Pay for advice separately if you need it. Do not conflate the two.

## The takeaway

SEBI's reforms are real and have saved investors meaningful money. But the single largest unnecessary cost most investors still pay is the regular plan markup. No reform needed — just a choice.
