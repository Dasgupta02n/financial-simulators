## The January rush

Every January, India's salaried class discovers Section 80C exists. By February, they are dumping money into whatever financial product will issue a receipt before March 31st. By April, they have forgotten about it until the next January.

This is not investment planning. This is tax-driven consumption.

## What 80C actually is

Section 80C allows you to deduct up to ₹1,50,000 from your taxable income under the old regime. Eligible instruments include PPF, ELSS, life insurance premiums, 5-year FDs, NSC, home loan principal repayment, and a few others.

The deduction reduces your tax liability. At the 30% slab, ₹1.5L in 80C saves you ₹46,800 (including cess). That's the benefit. Not ₹1.5L — just the tax on ₹1.5L.

## The problem with 80C-driven investing

When you invest to save tax, you optimise for the tax receipt, not the financial outcome. Common consequences:

**You buy life insurance you don't need.** Endowment policies and ULIPs are terrible investment vehicles — returning 4–6% over 15 years while equity funds return 10–12%. But they generate 80C receipts, so people buy them.

**You lock money in 5-year FDs at 7%.** After 30% tax on interest, that's 4.9% — below inflation. You saved tax on the principal but are losing it on the returns.

**You spread ₹1.5L across five instruments.** ₹30K in PPF, ₹30K in ELSS, ₹25K in insurance, ₹30K in NSC, ₹35K in FD. Each is too small to compound meaningfully. You've diversified mediocrity.

## What 80C should be

If you use the old regime, treat 80C as a tax tool, not an investment strategy. Pick the most efficient instrument for your situation:

- **PPF** if you want risk-free, tax-free returns (7.1% currently, fully exempt at maturity)
- **ELSS** if you want equity exposure with the shortest lock-in (3 years)
- **Home loan principal** if you already have one — it's the most painless 80C claim

Then invest the rest of your money based on your goals, not your tax deadline.

## The bottom line

Section 80C is a ₹46,800 tax benefit at best (for 30% slab taxpayers). It is not a retirement plan. It is not a wealth strategy. It is a line item on a tax return that expires every March. Use it efficiently or ignore it entirely — but don't structure your financial life around it.
