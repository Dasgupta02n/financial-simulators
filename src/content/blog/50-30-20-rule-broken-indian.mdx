## The rule that refuses to die

50% needs. 30% wants. 20% savings. Clean, memorable, universal. Also, for most Indian earners, impossible.

The 50-30-20 rule was popularized by Elizabeth Warren in the context of American household budgets. American salaries come with American cost structures. Indian CTCs come with something else entirely.

## The CTC problem

In India, your "salary" is not what you receive. Your CTC (Cost to Company) includes employer PF contribution, gratuity, leave encashment provisions, and sometimes insurance premiums. A ₹12 lakh CTC translates to roughly ₹75,000-80,000 in-hand per month, depending on your tax regime and PF structure.

Use the <CalculatorCard id="ctc-optimizer" /> to see your actual number. Most people are shocked by the gap between CTC and what reaches their bank account.

The 50-30-20 rule was designed for the in-hand number. Most Indians apply it to the CTC number. This alone makes the rule mathematically broken.

## Where the 50% goes in India

Your 50% "needs" bucket in India includes items the rule's creator never considered:

- **EMI for a house** — the average Indian household spends 30-40% of income on housing EMI. The rule allocates 50% for all needs combined. Your housing alone has consumed the entire needs budget.
- **Parental support** — in a country without social security, supporting aging parents is not optional. For most middle-class earners, this is ₹10,000-25,000/month.
- **Education loans** — the average education loan EMI for a Tier-1 engineering/MBA graduate is ₹15,000-25,000/month.
- **Health insurance and medical costs** — unlike countries with universal healthcare, Indians must privately fund both insurance and out-of-pocket medical costs.

After these, your "needs" are already at 60-70% of in-hand income. The 50% bucket overflowed before you bought groceries.

## What a functional Indian budget looks like

Instead of 50-30-20, try a framework that reflects Indian reality:

- **60% survival** — rent/EMI, groceries, utilities, transport, insurance, parental support, mandatory loan EMIs
- **15% future** — SIPs, PF, emergency fund, PPF. This is non-negotiable
- **15% near-term goals** — vacation, gadget upgrades, car maintenance, festival spending
- **10% guilt-free** — the amount you spend without tracking. Psychological necessity, not indulgence

## The key insight

The 50-30-20 rule's core wisdom is sound: automate savings before spending. Its proportions are wrong for India. The fix is simple — calculate your real in-hand income using the <CalculatorCard id="ctc-optimizer" />, then build your budget from that number, not your CTC. Save at least 15% before you allocate a single rupee to wants. The rest will sort itself out.
