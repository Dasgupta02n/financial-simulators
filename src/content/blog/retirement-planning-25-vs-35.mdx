There is a particular cruelty in compound interest: it rewards time far more generously than money. A person who invests ₹10,000 per month from age 25 to 35 and then stops entirely will end up with more money at 60 than someone who invests ₹10,000 per month from age 35 to 60. Same monthly amount. The first person pays for 10 years; the second pays for 25 years. The first person wins.

This is not a parable about discipline. It is a mathematical fact, and it is the single most important thing nobody tells you in your twenties because your twenties are occupied with more pressing concerns, such as whether the correct emoji has been deployed in a message to someone you find interesting.

## The numbers

Assume a 12% annual return (the approximate long-term Nifty 50 SIP return):

- **Person A**: ₹10,000/month from 25 to 35 (10 years), total invested: ₹12 lakh. Corpus at 60: approximately ₹1.62 crore.
- **Person B**: ₹10,000/month from 35 to 60 (25 years), total invested: ₹30 lakh. Corpus at 60: approximately ₹1.48 crore.

Person A invested ₹12 lakh and ended up with ₹1.62 crore. Person B invested ₹30 lakh and ended up with ₹1.48 crore. Person A spent ₹18 lakh less and still has ₹14 lakh more. The extra 10 years of compounding did what an additional ₹18 lakh of investment could not.

This is the cost of waiting. It is not ₹18 lakh. It is ₹18 lakh plus the 25 years of compounding that ₹18 lakh never received — a total opportunity cost that runs into tens of lakhs.

## Why the gap is larger than it appears

The example above assumes both persons invest the same nominal amount. In reality, the 25-year-old has a structural advantage that compounds further:

- **Income growth**: A 25-year-old's salary typically doubles every 5-7 years. By 35, their SIP can be ₹30,000-40,000/month, not ₹10,000.
- **Risk capacity**: At 25, a 100% equity allocation is appropriate. At 35, you may already be shifting toward balanced funds, reducing returns.
- **Time to recover**: A market crash at 27 gives you 33 years to recover. A market crash at 37 gives you 23 years. Same crash, different consequences.

If Person A increases their SIP by 10% per year (roughly matching salary growth), their corpus at 60 rises to approximately ₹4.5 crore. Person B, doing the same from 35, reaches approximately ₹2.2 crore. The gap is no longer ₹14 lakh. It is ₹2.3 crore — the price of a decade of delay.

## The psychological barrier

At 25, retirement is an abstraction. It lives in the same mental category as "buying a house" and "having children" — things that will happen in a distant, amorphous future that currently features none of them. The urgency is zero. The opportunity cost is invisible.

At 35, retirement has become a number. You have seen colleagues retire. You have felt the first twinge of a back that no longer considers standing up to be a simple operation. The urgency is suddenly very high. The opportunity cost is still invisible — because you cannot see what you would have had.

## The practical prescription

If you are 25: Start now. ₹5,000/month in a Nifty 50 index fund. Increase by 10% every year. Do this one thing, and you will have a retirement corpus that most 50-year-olds will envy. The SIP is cheaper than your phone bill and more valuable than anything your phone will ever show you.

If you are 35: The ship has not sailed, but it has left the dock. You must now invest more to achieve the same result. Use the Goal Planner above to calculate the monthly SIP required for your retirement target, given your starting age. The number will be larger than the one your 25-year-old self needed. That difference is the tuition fee for learning about compound interest a decade late.

If you are 45: The ship is in open water. You need aggressive savings (30-40% of income), high equity allocation, and a willingness to work until 62-65 rather than 55. The math is harsh but not hopeless — provided you start now rather than at 50.

Compound interest is the most powerful force in finance. It is also the most indifferent. It does not care about your excuses, your awareness, or your intentions. It cares only about when you started. The best time was 25. The second-best time is today.
