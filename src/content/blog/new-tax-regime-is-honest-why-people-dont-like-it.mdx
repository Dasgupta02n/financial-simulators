## The regime without illusions

The new tax regime has a simple proposition: pay lower rates, claim no deductions. It is, in theory, the most honest tax system India has ever had. There is nothing to optimise, nothing to scramble for before March 31st, nothing to explain to your CA.

And that is exactly why people don't like it.

## The psychology of deductions

Behavioural economists have a term for this: the "endowment effect." Once you have something — in this case, the ability to claim deductions — you value it more than its objective worth. Losing deductions feels like losing money, even if the new regime's lower rates give you more.

Consider: under the old regime, a ₹12L salary with ₹1.5L in 80C deductions saves you ₹31,200 in tax (at the 20% slab). Under the new regime, the same salary with zero deductions results in roughly the same or lower tax. The outcome is identical or better — but the process of "claiming" the deduction feels productive in a way that simply paying less tax does not.

## The industry around complexity

The old regime sustains an entire ecosystem: tax filing platforms, CAs who optimise deductions, ELSS funds that exist primarily for Section 80C, insurance agents selling endowment plans as "tax-saving instruments." The new regime makes most of this unnecessary. There is a reason this ecosystem does not enthusiastically promote it.

## When the new regime is clearly better

If you claim fewer than ₹3.5L in total deductions (80C + 80D + HRA + home loan interest + NPS), the new regime almost certainly saves you money. This describes the majority of taxpayers earning between ₹7L and ₹15L — people who may have 80C but don't have home loans or significant HRA.

## When the old regime still wins

If you have a home loan (₹2L interest deduction), maximise 80C (₹1.5L), claim 80D (₹25K), and contribute to NPS (₹50K), that's ₹4.25L in deductions. At ₹15L+ salary, the old regime is mathematically superior. But this describes a specific type of taxpayer: mid-career, home-owning, with structured investments.

## The honest calculation

The question is not "which regime is better." The question is "which regime is better for you." That depends on exactly one variable: your total deductions. Not your feelings about deductions. Not your CA's preference. Not your father's advice from 2008.

## The bottom line

The new regime is honest about what it costs. The old regime lets you feel clever about what you save. Cleverness has a price — it's called spending money on things you wouldn't otherwise buy, just to claim a deduction. The new regime asks you to evaluate investments on their merits. That discomfort you feel? That's the absence of a crutch.
