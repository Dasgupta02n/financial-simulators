## The returns that make headlines

Over the 3 years ending March 2026, the BSE Small Cap index returned approximately 22% CAGR. Several small-cap mutual funds posted 25-30% annual returns. These numbers dominate financial media and trigger the usual flood of new fund offers.

Before you allocate, consider what is actually happening.

## The liquidity trap

Small-cap stocks in India have low free floats and thin trading volumes. SEBI defines small-cap as the 251st to 500th company by market capitalisation on the exchanges. Many of these companies have daily trading volumes under ₹5 crore.

When a mutual fund with ₹15,000 crore AUM tries to enter or exit a small-cap stock, it moves the price. The fund cannot actually buy at the price it sees on screen if it needs to acquire a meaningful position. It must buy in tranches over weeks, often pushing the price up as it goes. This is called market impact cost, and it is not captured in the index return.

The index returned 22%. The average investor in a small-cap fund captured perhaps 17-18% after impact costs, tracking error, and the fund's own expense ratio. The gap is structural, not accidental.

## The survivorship problem, again

The small-cap index is rebalanced periodically. Stocks that decline sharply or are delisted are removed and replaced by stocks that have risen into the rank range. The index's historical return is computed on the stocks that survived and thrived. It does not reflect the companies that went bankrupt, were delisted, or simply stagnated into irrelevance.

A 2024 study by a leading mutual fund analytics firm found that roughly 18% of small-cap stocks in the BSE Small Cap index over a 10-year period either were delisted or fell more than 80%. The index return excludes most of these outcomes.

## The redemption problem

Small-cap funds face a structural mismatch: they offer daily liquidity while holding illiquid assets. If a significant number of investors redeem simultaneously — as happened in early 2024 when SEBI expressed concerns about small-cap fund valuations — the fund must sell into a falling market. The first investors to redeem get full NAV. The last ones bear the impact cost of the entire group's exit.

This is not hypothetical. In Q1 2024, several small-cap funds imposed limits on redemptions or restricted fresh inflows precisely because the liquidity mismatch was becoming unmanageable.

## What you should actually do

Small-cap allocation should be 10-15% of your equity portfolio at most. Treat it as a satellite allocation, not the core. A ₹5 lakh lump sum invested in a small-cap fund at a market peak can take 3-4 years to recover. The same amount via monthly SIPs over 2 years smooths your entry and dramatically reduces the risk of buying at the top.

## The honest conclusion

Small-cap returns are real but partially illusory. The headline number overstates what most investors actually capture due to impact costs, survivorship bias, and liquidity constraints. Allocate modestly, enter gradually, and never treat small-cap returns as representative of what you will actually earn.
