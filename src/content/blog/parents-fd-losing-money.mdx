## The safest place for money (that isn't)

Your parents have ₹40 lakh in fixed deposits. The bank gives them 7.25% for senior citizens. They feel secure. The number on the passbook goes up every quarter. What could be wrong?

Quite a lot, as it happens.

## The quiet erosion

The math is straightforward but the conclusion is uncomfortable:

- **FD rate for senior citizens** — approximately 7.25% (SBI, HDFC as of early 2026)
- **Income tax** — for a senior citizen in the 20% tax bracket, post-tax return is 7.25% minus 20% = 5.8%. In the 30% bracket, it drops to 5.075%.
- **Inflation** — CPI inflation at approximately 5-6%. For healthcare, which dominates senior citizen spending, inflation runs at 14%.

In the 20% tax bracket, the real (inflation-adjusted) return on that FD is approximately 5.8% minus 5.5% general inflation = **0.3%**. For medical expenses, the real return is 5.8% minus 14% = **-8.2%**.

Your parents' ₹40 lakh is growing in nominal terms and shrinking in purchasing power. The passbook shows a larger number that buys less.

## Why they do it anyway

Your parents are not being irrational. They are optimizing for a risk that is very real to them: the risk of losing capital. They watched the 1991 economic crisis. They remember when banks failed and when equity markets crashed 50%. For them, the FD is not an investment — it is a guarantee that the principal will exist tomorrow.

This is a legitimate concern. The solution is not to tell them to put everything in equity. The solution is to gently show them that "safe" has a cost, and then offer a middle path.

## The conversation framework

1. **Start with medical inflation** — "Amma, a cataract surgery that cost ₹30,000 five years ago now costs ₹55,000. Your FD is earning enough for groceries but not for hospital bills that grow three times faster."

2. **Show, don't tell** — Run the <CalculatorCard id="fd-comparator" /> with their actual numbers. The post-inflation, post-tax number is more persuasive than any lecture.

3. **Propose a gradual shift** — Move 20-30% of the FD corpus into senior citizen savings schemes (SCSS at 8.2%), post office monthly income schemes, or short-duration debt funds. These are not equity. They are simply better versions of what they already use.

4. **Keep the emergency portion in FDs** — 12-18 months of expenses should remain in FDs or savings accounts. The rest can earn more.

## The hard number

Over 10 years, ₹40 lakh in FDs at 7.25% (20% tax bracket) grows to approximately ₹70.4 lakh nominal. After 5.5% inflation, that ₹70.4 lakh has the purchasing power of approximately ₹41.4 lakh in today's money. Ten years of patience for ₹1.4 lakh of real gain on ₹40 lakh. That is a real return of 0.3% per year.

A mixed portfolio — 50% FDs, 30% SCSS/PMVVY, 20% balanced advantage fund — would likely yield 2-3% real returns after tax. Not exciting. But three to ten times better than what they have now.
