## The number that isn't yours

When a company offers you a ₹15 lakh CTC, they are not offering you ₹15 lakh. They are offering you a number that includes their contribution to your PF, your gratuity, your medical insurance, and occasionally the office coffee machine. None of this hits your bank account.

Let's dissect a typical ₹15L CTC.

## The anatomy of a ₹15 lakh CTC

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Basic salary | ₹5,00,000 |
| HRA | ₹2,50,000 |
| Special allowance | ₹3,50,000 |
| Employer PF (12% of basic) | ₹72,000 |
| Gratuity | ₹38,500 |
| Medical insurance | ₹15,000 |
| Variable pay | ₹2,74,500 |

That "CTC" includes ₹1,25,500 that you never touch — employer PF, gratuity, and insurance. Your gross salary is ₹13,74,500. After tax and your own PF deduction (₹60,000), your in-hand is roughly ₹10,80,000 for the year. That's **₹90,000 per month** — not the ₹1,25,000 the CTC number implied.

## The variable pay illusion

Variable pay — that cheerful ₹2.74L in the example — is contingent on performance, company targets, and whether the finance department is having a good quarter. In most companies, the actual payout is 70–90% of target. Budget for 80% and you'll rarely be disappointed.

## PF: the forced savings you can't touch

Your employer contributes 12% of basic to PF. You match it. That's ₹1,44,000/year locked away until retirement (or a very specific set of withdrawal conditions). It's not a bad thing — it's a reasonable retirement safety net — but it is not money you can use to pay rent this month.

## Why HR structures CTC this way

Companies maximise CTC because it's the number that wins the talent market. A higher CTC attracts candidates. A lower in-hand salary retains them through confusion. The system is not broken by accident.
