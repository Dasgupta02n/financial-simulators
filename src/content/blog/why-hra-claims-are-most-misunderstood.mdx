## The most casually claimed deduction

"I pay rent, so I get HRA exemption." This is the working assumption for most salaried people in India. It is, at best, partially correct.

HRA exemption is not a flat amount. It is the **minimum** of three calculations, and most people only know one of them.

## The three-part formula

Your HRA exemption is the **least** of:

1. **Actual HRA received** from your employer
2. **Rent paid minus 10% of basic salary**
3. **50% of basic salary** (metro cities) or **40% of basic salary** (non-metro)

Let's say your basic salary is ₹5L/year, you receive ₹2.4L HRA, and you pay ₹3.6L rent in Mumbai.

- Actual HRA received: ₹2,40,000
- Rent paid – 10% of basic: ₹3,60,000 – ₹50,000 = ₹3,10,000
- 50% of basic (metro): ₹2,50,000

Your exemption is the minimum: **₹2,40,000**. The actual HRA received becomes the limiting factor. Paying more rent doesn't help you.

## The metro trap

Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata, Chennai = 50% of basic. Everything else = 40% of basic. If you live in Bengaluru, Hyderabad, or Pune — cities with rents that rival Mumbai — you get the 40% calculation, not the 50% one. The government's list of "metro cities" was written in 1994 and hasn't been updated since.

## The self-owned house problem

If you own the house you live in, you cannot claim HRA — even if you're paying EMI on it. You can, however, claim home loan interest deduction under Section 24(b) up to ₹2L. If you're renting while also paying a home loan for a different property, you can claim both HRA and Section 24(b). Many people don't.

## What your HR department gets wrong

Most companies ask for a rent amount and calculate HRA exemption based on that. But if your declared rent is higher than the formula allows, you're not getting the full benefit — and you may be declaring income you don't need to. If your rent is lower than the formula, you're leaving money on the table.

## The bottom line

HRA exemption is not automatic. It is a function of three variables, and the smallest one wins. Know which one limits your claim before you sign a lease or fill out your tax declaration. And if you're in a non-metro city, accept that the tax code thinks your rent is 20% less real than a Mumbaikar's.
