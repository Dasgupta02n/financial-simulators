## The concept that changes everything

Most financial advice in India operates in nominal terms. "This FD gives 7%." "This SIP returned 14%." "Your salary grew 8% this year." These numbers are all incomplete. They describe what happened to your money. They do not describe what happened to your money's ability to buy things.

That gap is inflation. And until you understand it, every other financial concept is built on sand.

## The ₹100 note over time

A ₹100 note in 2015 could buy approximately what ₹170 buys in 2026. In eleven years, the rupee lost approximately 41% of its purchasing power at a compounded 5.5% annual inflation rate. The note still says ₹100. The printer did not change it. But the shopkeeper did.

This is not abstract. It is the reason your parents' pension feels smaller every year. It is the reason the auto fare that was ₹8/km in 2015 is now ₹18-25/km. It is the reason the ₹50,000 salary that felt comfortable in 2018 now feels tight. Nothing about your finances changed. The rupee changed underneath you.

## How inflation distorts every financial decision

**Fixed deposits** — A 7% FD sounds like a return. After 5.5% inflation and 20% tax, it is a 0.3% real return. You are essentially lending money to the bank at zero profit to preserve capital nominally while losing purchasing power.

**Salary negotiations** — An 8% raise at 5.5% inflation is a 2.5% real increase. If your industry's inflation (education costs, healthcare, housing) runs higher than CPI, you may be getting poorer despite the raise.

**Property** — "Real estate always goes up" is true only nominally. Indian residential property in most Tier-1 cities has appreciated at approximately 5-7% annually over the last decade. After inflation, that is 0-1.5% real appreciation. You are not getting richer from your flat. You are keeping pace, barely.

**SIPs** — A 12% equity SIP return sounds impressive. After 5.5% inflation, it is approximately 6.2% real. That is the number that determines whether you reach your financial goals. ## Why India's inflation is harder to see

India's CPI basket weights food at approximately 45%. If you spend a larger share of your income on education, healthcare, and housing — as most urban middle-class families do — your personal inflation rate is higher than the official CPI. Education inflation runs at 10-12% annually. Healthcare at 13-14%. These are not optional expenditures. They are the core costs of a middle-class life, and they compound faster than the headline number suggests.

## What understanding inflation actually changes

Once you internalize inflation, several things become clear:

1. **Cash is not safe** — keeping money in a savings account or low-yield FD is not "playing it safe." It is a guaranteed loss of purchasing power. Safety is relative. If inflation is 5.5%, any instrument returning below 5.5% post-tax is destroying your wealth, however predictably.

2. **Returns must be stated in real terms** — always subtract inflation before comparing options. A 7% FD and a 12% equity fund are not 5% apart. After 5.5% inflation, they are 1.5% versus 6.5% real — a 4x difference in actual wealth creation.

3. **Time is not neutral** — a 10-year goal and a 20-year goal are not the same goal with different deadlines. At 5.5% inflation, the 20-year goal requires nearly double the nominal target. This changes your entire savings plan.

Financial literacy in India typically begins with "save money." It should begin with "understand what money is losing." The rest follows naturally.
