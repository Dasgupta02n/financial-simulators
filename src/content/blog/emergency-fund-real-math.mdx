## The advice everyone gives

"Keep 3 to 6 months of expenses in your emergency fund."

This is the personal finance equivalent of telling someone to drink water. Technically correct, utterly insufficient.

## Why the rule undershoots

Consider what an emergency in India actually looks like. It is rarely one tidy event. A job loss often coincides with a medical episode. A parent's hospitalization often coincides with a home repair you cannot defer. Emergencies, in the Indian experience, arrive not as single visitors but as delegations.

Now consider the numbers:

- **Medical inflation in India** runs at approximately 14% annually (RBI and insurance industry data), roughly double general CPI inflation. A hospitalization that cost ₹2 lakh five years ago now costs ₹4 lakh. Your "6-month fund" was calculated using yesterday's bills.
- **Average job search duration** in Indian metros for mid-senior roles: 4 to 7 months (Naukri and LinkedIn data). The lower end of the "6 months" advice assumes you find work on the exact day your fund runs dry. That is optimism posing as math.
- **Income gaps in freelance/gig work** — for the growing cohort without salaried jobs, a "month" of zero income can become three without warning.

## The real calculation

Take your actual monthly burn rate — not your salary, but what you spend if you cut all discretionary spending. Include EMI obligations, school fees, insurance premiums, and the minimum groceries and utilities to keep the household running. Multiply that by 9. Not 3, not 6. Nine.

Then adjust for two things:

1. **Add ₹3-5 lakh for a medical buffer** beyond what your insurance covers. Most Indian health insurance policies have room rent caps, co-pays, and exclusions that leave 20-30% of actual hospital bills out of pocket.

2. **Subtract what you can liquidate in 48 hours** — a fixed deposit, a liquid fund, a savings account balance. These are your actual emergency fund, not theoretical.

## Where to keep it

An emergency fund that earns 3.5% in a savings account while inflation runs at 5-6% is an emergency fund that shrinks by 2.5% annually. The entire purpose of the fund is preservation and access, not growth — but that does not mean you should volunteer for negative real returns.

The difference over 3-5 years is not trivial. A ₹5 lakh fund losing 2.5% real per year for five years loses roughly ₹60,000 in purchasing power. That is another month of expenses you no longer have.

## The honest framing

Your emergency fund is not an investment. It is insurance that you fund yourself. Like all insurance, it should be adequate to the risk. In India, the risk is higher than the textbooks assume — higher medical costs, longer job searches, thinner social safety nets. Build for the world you live in, not the one in the financial advice brochure.
