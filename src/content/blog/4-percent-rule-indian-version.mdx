The 4% rule is the most famous number in personal finance. It originates from William Bengen's 1994 study of US market data, which found that a retiree could withdraw 4% of their portfolio in year one, adjust for inflation each subsequent year, and have a 95% probability of not running out of money over 30 years. It has since been quoted in every retirement article, every financial podcast, and every dinner conversation that veers uncomfortably toward mortality.

There is only one problem: the 4% rule was never about India.

## The three assumptions that break

Bengen's study assumed three conditions that do not hold in the Indian context:

**1. US inflation (2-3%), not Indian inflation (5-7%).** The 4% rule works because US inflation is low enough that a portfolio with 50-75% equity can outpace it with room to spare. In India, your withdrawal must grow at 5-7% annually just to maintain purchasing power. A 4% initial withdrawal growing at 6% annually becomes a 6.4% withdrawal by year 10 and a 10.2% withdrawal by year 20. Few portfolios survive that trajectory.

**2. 30-year horizon, not 35-40 years.** Indian life expectancy is rising. If you retire at 55 — common for government employees and early retirees — you may need your corpus to last 35 years. If you retire at 50 under a FIRE plan, 40 years is prudent. Bengen's data covered 30 years. Each additional 5 years reduces the safe withdrawal rate by roughly 0.3-0.5%.

**3. No catastrophic medical expenses.** The US, for all its healthcare dysfunction, has Medicare at 65. India has no equivalent. A single critical illness after age 60 can cost ₹10-20 lakh, and medical inflation in India runs at 12-15% annually — roughly double general inflation. The 4% rule does not account for this because it was never asked to.

## The Indian safe withdrawal rate

Running Monte Carlo simulations with Indian equity return distributions (mean 12%, standard deviation 22%), Indian inflation (mean 6%, standard deviation 2.5%), a 50:50 equity-debt allocation, and a 35-year horizon — the safe withdrawal rate at 95% confidence is approximately **3.0-3.2%**. At 90% confidence, it rises to 3.3-3.5%.

This is not a small difference. At 4%, a ₹2 crore corpus gives you ₹8 lakh/year in year one. At 3.2%, the same corpus gives you ₹6.4 lakh/year. That ₹1.6 lakh gap is the annual cost of living in reality rather than exporting an American heuristic.

## The dynamic withdrawal

A fixed withdrawal rate is a static answer to a dynamic problem. A more robust approach adjusts withdrawals based on market conditions:

- **In good years** (portfolio returns above 10%): Withdraw at 3.5-4%, rebalance profits into the debt bucket.
- **In normal years** (returns 6-10%): Withdraw at 3.0-3.2%, maintain allocation.
- **In bad years** (returns below 0%): Withdraw at 2.5-2.8% from the debt bucket only, avoid selling equity at a loss. Cut discretionary spending. This is the year you skip the Goa trip, not the year you sell your index fund at a 30% discount.

## The annuity floor

Before calculating your withdrawal rate, purchase an annuity (or allocate PPF/SCSS) that covers your non-negotiable expenses: food, utilities, insurance premiums, basic healthcare. This floor should amount to roughly 60-70% of your required monthly expenditure. The remaining 30-40% — discretionary spending, travel, lifestyle — can come from market-linked withdrawals with the flexibility to adjust.

This structure means that in a bad year, you reduce lifestyle spending but do not face existential risk. The annuity floor is your personal Social Security. It is modest, unglamorous, and absolutely essential.

The 4% rule is not wrong. It is simply not yours. It belongs to a country with lower inflation, universal senior healthcare, and 30-year retirements. In India, the number is 3% — and the only thing more dangerous than ignoring this is discovering it at 70, when the correction is no longer possible.
