The average Indian retiree has enough savings to last approximately 7 years. The average Indian retiree will live approximately 18-20 years after retirement. The gap — 11-13 years of unfunded existence — is the single largest financial failure hiding in plain sight in the Indian middle class. It is not discussed because it is not marketed. There is no mutual fund SIP that advertises "you will probably run out of money at 72."

## Where the 7 years come from

The median retirement corpus in India, across all salaried employees with EPF coverage, is approximately ₹15-20 lakh. This is not a typo. The EPF wage ceiling was ₹6,500 until 2014 and ₹15,000 thereafter. Most EPF accounts, even after 30 years of contributions, accumulate ₹25-40 lakh. Add voluntary PF, PPF, some FDs, and perhaps a small SIP, and the median salaried retiree sits on ₹40-60 lakh.

At ₹25,000 per month in expenses (a conservative estimate for a Tier-2 city), ₹60 lakh lasts 20 years — if you keep it under your mattress. But you do not keep it under your mattress; you spend it, and inflation spends it faster. At 6% inflation, your ₹25,000/month becomes ₹50,000/month in 12 years. Your corpus does not double. Your expenses do.

The result: ₹60 lakh, spent at an inflation-adjusted rate, lasts approximately 7-9 years. Not 20. Not 15. Seven to nine.

## The missing 13 years

Why is the gap so large? Three reasons, each structural and none easily fixed.

**First, Indian savings are concentrated in real estate.** The average Indian household holds 77% of its wealth in real estate (RBI survey, 2023). Real estate is illiquid, appreciates at roughly 5-6% nominally (barely matching inflation), and cannot be consumed piecemeal for monthly expenses. You cannot sell one bedroom per year. The house is wealth; it is not retirement income.

**Second, EPF coverage is shallow.** Only 6.5 crore Indians have EPF accounts in a workforce of 55 crore. The informal sector — 90% of Indian workers — has no mandated retirement savings. Even among the formal sector, the wage ceiling means contributions are capped at levels that cannot generate adequate corpus.

**Third, there is no social security.** The Atal Pension Yojana, the closest thing India has to a universal pension, provides a maximum of ₹5,000/month — a number that was inadequate when the scheme launched in 2015 and is approximately 40% less adequate now. There is no Medicare, no state pension indexed to inflation, no safety net that catches you when your corpus runs out.

## The generation most at risk

This crisis falls heaviest on those currently aged 40-55. They entered the workforce in the 1990s, when EPF was the only option and equity was "speculation." They saved in EPF and PPF, bought a flat, and assumed the combination would suffice. Nobody told them that a ₹30 lakh flat in 2000, now worth ₹1.2 crore, is still one house — and you need one house to live in. The appreciation is theoretical unless you sell and downgrade, which most people will not.

The younger generation (25-35) has access to SIPs, index funds, and better financial education. But even among them, the savings rate is 15-20% of income, while the required rate for adequate retirement (assuming no social security) is 30-35%. The math is unforgiving regardless of generation.

## What to do now

If you are 40 with ₹20 lakh in retirement savings, you are not behind — you are the median. The median is simply not enough. Use the calculator above to determine the gap between your current trajectory and your required corpus. Then:

- Increase your equity allocation. Over 15-20 years, equity is not risk; the absence of equity is risk.
- Maximize NPS for the additional ₹50,000 deduction. It is nearly free money for taxpayers.
- Start a parallel SIP in an index fund. Even ₹10,000/month at 12% for 20 years produces approximately ₹1 crore.
- Do not count your house as retirement savings. It is shelter, not income.

Seven years of savings is not a retirement plan. It is a countdown timer. The difference between recognizing this at 45 and discovering it at 68 is the difference between a correction and a catastrophe.
