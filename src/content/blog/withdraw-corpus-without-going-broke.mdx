Accumulating a retirement corpus is a problem of discipline. Withdrawing from it is a problem of strategy. India's financial ecosystem has written approximately ten thousand guides on how to save for retirement. It has written roughly four on how to spend in retirement. This asymmetry explains a great deal about why people who saved diligently still go broke at 75.

## The deaccumulation dilemma

During accumulation, market downturns are your friend — you buy more units at lower prices. During deaccumulation, market downturns are your enemy — you sell units at lower prices to fund expenses, permanently reducing the base that will recover. This asymmetry, called sequence-of-returns risk, is the single most underappreciated risk in Indian retirement planning.

Consider: a ₹3 crore corpus with a 4% withdrawal rate (₹12 lakh/year). If the market drops 30% in year one, your corpus falls to ₹2.1 crore minus ₹12 lakh withdrawal = ₹1.98 crore. Even if the market recovers 30% in year two, your corpus rises to ₹2.57 crore — not back to ₹3 crore, because you withdrew from a diminished base. This hole deepens every year you withdraw during a downturn.

## The bucket strategy

The most robust withdrawal framework for Indian conditions divides your corpus into three buckets:

**Bucket 1 — Immediate (Years 1-5):** Liquid and short-duration debt funds, FD ladders, savings account. This is your spending money. It earns 5-6% and exists so you never sell equity in a downturn. Target: 4-5 years of expenses. For ₹12 lakh annual withdrawal, this is ₹48-60 lakh.

**Bucket 2 — Medium (Years 5-15):** A mix of corporate bonds, PPF (if accessible), and balanced advantage funds. This bucket rebalances from equity when markets are up and replenishes Bucket 1. Target: 10 years of expenses.

**Bucket 3 — Long-term (Years 15+):** Pure equity — index funds, flexi-cap funds. This bucket has the longest time horizon and the highest return potential. It exists to fund the later years of retirement when inflation has eroded the purchasing power of your earlier buckets.

## Inflation-adjusted withdrawals

Do not withdraw a fixed nominal amount. Withdraw a fixed *real* amount, adjusted for inflation each year. If you need ₹12 lakh in year one and inflation is 6%, withdraw ₹12.72 lakh in year two. This sounds obvious. Most people do not do it, because it feels like spending more. What is actually happening is that you are spending the same — the rupee is simply worth less.

## The medical buffer

Separate your medical expenses from your regular withdrawal. Indian medical inflation runs at 12-15% annually. If you include medical costs in your base withdrawal rate, the rising medical expenses will cannibalize your lifestyle budget within a decade. Instead, maintain a separate health corpus — ideally in a combination of super-top-up health insurance (for catastrophic events) and a dedicated medical fund (for chronic, non-hospitalized expenses that insurance does not cover).

## The rule of thirds

A practical heuristic for Indian retirees: spend one-third of your corpus in the first third of retirement, one-third in the second, and one-third in the third. This implicitly accounts for inflation (later withdrawals are larger in nominal terms) and longevity risk (you do not front-load spending).

If your corpus is ₹3 crore and you expect a 27-year retirement, target roughly ₹1 crore per 9-year block. Year one withdrawal: approximately ₹8-9 lakh, growing with inflation. This translates to a starting withdrawal rate of roughly 2.8-3%, which is conservative — and conservatism in retirement planning is the quality that separates the solvent from the anxious.

Retirement withdrawal is not a problem you solve once. It is a problem you manage continuously, like blood pressure, for the rest of your life.
