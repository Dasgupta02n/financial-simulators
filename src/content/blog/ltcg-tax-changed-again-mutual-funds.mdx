## The tax that keeps moving

Long-Term Capital Gains tax on equity mutual funds has changed three times in four years. Before 2018, it was zero. Then it became 10% above ₹1L. Budget 2024 moved it to 12.5% above ₹1.25L. The rules have now stabilised — for the moment.

Here's what the current regime means for your SIP returns.

## Current LTCG rules (FY 2026-27)

- Holding period for equity funds: >12 months = long-term
- LTCG rate: 12.5% on gains above ₹1,25,000 per year
- Short-term capital gains (STCG) on equity: 20% (up from 15%)
- No indexation benefit for equity

The exemption of ₹1.25L per financial year means the first ₹1.25L of profit you book is tax-free. After that, 12.5% goes to the government.

## A real example

You invested ₹5L in an equity fund three years ago. It's now worth ₹8L. Gain: ₹3L. Tax calculation:

- First ₹1.25L: exempt
- Remaining ₹1.75L: taxed at 12.5% = ₹21,875

Your effective tax rate on the total gain: 7.3%. Not terrible. But not zero, which is what it was before 2018.

## The SIP problem

SIP investors face a peculiar issue. Each monthly instalment has its own purchase date and its own gain. When you redeem, you typically redeem the oldest units first (FIFO). This means most of your redeemed units are long-term — but if you started the SIP less than a year ago, some units will attract STCG at 20%, which is a substantially worse deal.

If you're running a ₹20,000/month SIP and redeeming after 14 months, roughly 2 months' worth of units (₹40,000) will attract STCG. On a ₹8,000 gain, that's ₹1,600 in tax instead of ₹1,000 under LTCG.

## What changed versus the old rules

Under the pre-2024 regime (10% above ₹1L), the same ₹3L gain would have cost ₹20,000 in tax. Now it costs ₹21,875. The difference is ₹1,875 — not life-changing, but not nothing when you scale it over decades of compounding.

## The bottom line

LTCG tax on equity is a moderate, not catastrophic, drag. At 12.5% above ₹1.25L, it's lower than most countries' capital gains rates. The real danger isn't the tax — it's forgetting to account for it when planning redemptions. Book gains strategically, use the annual exemption, and don't let short-term units pile up if you can help it.
