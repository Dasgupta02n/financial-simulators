## The deduction hiding in plain sight

Section 80CCD(1B) allows an additional ₹50,000 deduction for NPS contributions — over and above the ₹1.5L limit under Section 80C. It is the only deduction in the Indian tax code that sits entirely outside the 80C umbrella.

And roughly 85% of eligible taxpayers don't use it.

## Why people skip it

The reasons are predictable:

**"NPS locks my money until 60."** Partially true. You can withdraw 25% of contributions after 3 years for specific reasons (home, education, marriage, illness). At 60, you can withdraw 60% tax-free; the remaining 40% must buy an annuity.

**"The returns aren't great."** NPS equity funds have returned 11–13% annually over the last 5 years. The conservative government securities fund returns 7–8%. These are reasonable, not spectacular — but they're not terrible either.

**"I don't want another account."** NPS requires a PRAN (Permanent Retirement Account Number) and a minimal contribution of ₹1,000/year. It is bureaucratically simple to open — you can do it online in 15 minutes through eNPS.

**"My employer doesn't offer it."** You don't need your employer. Any individual can open an NPS account independently.

## The actual tax benefit

At the 30% slab (with 4% cess), ₹50,000 in NPS saves you **₹15,600 per year**. That's ₹1,300/month in tax savings. Over 10 years, if you contribute ₹50K annually and earn 10% returns, you accumulate roughly ₹8.7L — of which ₹5L is your contribution and ₹3.7L is market returns.

Under the new regime, 80CCD(1B) doesn't apply. This deduction exists only under the old regime — which is another reason the new regime's simplicity comes at a cost for diligent tax planners.

## The employer NPS benefit

Under Section 80CCD(2), your employer's NPS contribution (up to 14% of basic for central government, 10% for others) is deductible — and this applies under the **new regime** as well. If your employer offers NPS as part of CTC, this is one of the few deductions available under both regimes.

A ₹1L employer NPS contribution at the 30% slab saves ₹31,200 — and you don't sacrifice anything from your salary to get it.

## The annuity problem at retirement

When you retire, 40% of your NPS corpus must buy an annuity. Annuity rates in India are currently 6–7% — and the annuity income is taxable. This is the legitimate concern about NPS: the forced annuity at moderate rates with taxable income. Plan for this. The 60% tax-free lump sum partially compensates.

## The bottom line

₹50,000 under 80CCD(1B) saves ₹15,600/year for old regime taxpayers in the 30% slab. Over 20 years, the compounding of contributions and returns builds a meaningful retirement corpus. The lock-in is real but not absolute, and the returns are reasonable. The biggest risk isn't NPS — it's leaving ₹15,600 on the table every year because opening one more account feels like too much work.
