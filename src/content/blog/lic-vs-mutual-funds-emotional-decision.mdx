## The question that is not really a question

Ask most Indian families whether LIC or mutual funds are better, and you are not starting a financial discussion. You are trespassing on emotional territory. LIC is what your father bought. It is what your uncle recommends at every family gathering. It is synonymous with safety in a way that no mutual fund can match, regardless of what the numbers say.

Let us look at the numbers anyway.

## The return comparison

LIC's average policy return over the last decade is roughly 5-6.5% depending on the plan and bonus declarations. This is not a secret — it is in their annual reports. The LIC Jeevan Labh plan, among their more popular endowment products, has declared bonuses that translate to approximately 5.8% IRR for a 25-year policy.

A diversified equity mutual fund has returned roughly 11-13% CAGR over the same period. A balanced advantage fund — which automatically manages equity-debt allocation and carries lower volatility — has returned roughly 9-10%.

Over 25 years, the difference is staggering. A ₹5,000 monthly premium into LIC at 5.8% yields approximately ₹35.5 lakh. The same amount into a balanced advantage fund at 9.5% yields approximately ₹62.8 lakh. The gap is ₹27.3 lakh — not from higher risk, but from a higher-returning asset class held for a long period.

## But what about the insurance?

Here is the key point that LIC's emotional appeal rests on: "You are getting insurance plus savings." True. But you are paying for both in a single product where you cannot see what each costs.

A ₹10 lakh term insurance plan from a private insurer for a 30-year-old costs roughly ₹8,000-12,000 per year. The remaining amount — say ₹50,000 minus ₹10,000 = ₹40,000 — can be invested in a mutual fund. This "buy term and invest the rest" approach gives you the same insurance cover and a significantly higher corpus.

LIC's endowment plans effectively bundle a ₹10,000-12,000 insurance charge with a savings component returning 5-6%. You would not buy a phone and a refrigerator as a single product. Financial products deserve the same scrutiny.

## The real reason people choose LIC

It is not ignorance. It is risk perception shaped by lived experience. Many Indians over 50 watched their parents lose money in the 1992 securities scam, or saw the 2008 crash destroy portfolios. LIC never missed a bonus payment. It is backed by the Government of India. In a country where bank failures, chit fund scams, and Ponzi schemes have eroded trust repeatedly, the certainty of LIC — however modest — feels different from the promised returns of a mutual fund.

This is a rational emotional response. But it is not a rational financial one.

## The honest middle path

If you cannot bring yourself to invest entirely in mutual funds, split the difference. Keep your existing LIC policies — surrendering them is usually a bad idea due to low surrender values. For new investments, buy term insurance and invest the rest in a balanced fund. You honour the emotional need for safety through the term cover and the mathematical need for growth through the mutual fund.

No financial plan survives contact with human psychology. But a good plan at least makes the trade-offs visible.
