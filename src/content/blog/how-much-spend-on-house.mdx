## What the bank says you can afford

Banks in India will approve a home loan EMI of up to 40-50% of your net monthly income. This is not generosity. It is the maximum pain they calculate you can endure while still making payments.

That you can endure something does not mean you should.

## The real cost of a ₹50 lakh home

Let us trace the complete cost of buying a ₹50 lakh flat in a Tier-1 Indian city:

- **Down payment (20%)** — ₹10 lakh from savings
- **Loan amount** — ₹40 lakh at 8.5% for 20 years
- **EMI** — ₹34,700/month
- **Total amount paid over 20 years** — ₹83.3 lakh
- **Interest paid** — ₹43.3 lakh (more than the original loan)

You bought a ₹50 lakh flat. You paid ₹93.3 lakh for it (down payment + EMI total). And that is before registration (5-7% of property value, approximately ₹3 lakh), stamp duty, interior work (₹5-10 lakh), and society maintenance (₹3,000-8,000/month forever).

## The ratio that matters

Instead of the bank's 40-50% EMI-to-income ratio, use this framework:

- **25-30% of in-hand income** — this is the EMI you can sustain without sacrificing all other financial goals. At ₹1 lakh in-hand, that is ₹25,000-30,000/month.
- **Home price ≤ 4x annual in-hand income** — earning ₹12 lakh in-hand per year? Your ceiling is ₹48 lakh. This is unfashionable in Indian metros. That is precisely why it is correct.
- **Maintain a 6-month EMI buffer** — in addition to your emergency fund. Job losses in India's IT and startup sectors can be abrupt. You need a dedicated EMI survival fund.

## The opportunity cost nobody mentions

Every rupee locked in EMI is a rupee not invested. A ₹30,000 EMI for 20 years means ₹72 lakh that cannot be invested elsewhere. Had half that amount gone into equity SIPs at 12% annualized, it would grow to approximately ₹74 lakh in 20 years. You chose a flat that appreciated 5-7% annually (Indian residential real estate's long-term average in most cities) over an asset that compounded at 12%. The math is not subtle.

## When buying actually makes sense

Buy a house when:
- The EMI is under 30% of in-hand income
- You plan to stay in the property for 10+ years
- The rent-vs-buy math (including all costs) favors buying for your specific location
- You already have an emergency fund and are investing 15%+ of income

Until then, rent. Renting in Indian cities costs 2-3% of property value annually. Your EMI costs 8.5% of the loan amount plus the opportunity cost of the down payment. Renting is not "throwing money away." It is buying financial flexibility at a known price.
