8.25%. That is the Employees' Provident Fund interest rate for FY 2024-25, and it has the comforting quality of a number that looks larger than inflation. For over two decades, EPF has hovered between 8% and 8.75%, creating the impression that your retirement savings are not merely safe but productive. This impression is approximately half-true, which in engineering is called a failure and in finance is called a portfolio.

## The real return

If inflation is 5-6%, a nominal return of 8.25% gives you a real return of 2.25-3.25%. This is not catastrophic. It is also not sufficient. Over 30 years, a real return of 3% means your money doubles in purchasing power roughly every 24 years. If you start with ₹10 lakh in EPF at 25, by 55 it has the purchasing power of roughly ₹20 lakh in today's terms. That sounds like growth. It is growth the way a plant growing one inch per year is technically alive — accurate, but not enough to shade you from anything.

## The accumulation problem

EPF contributions are capped at 12% of basic salary, with a wage ceiling of ₹15,000 per month (above which it is optional). That means the mandatory contribution is ₹1,800/month from employee + ₹1,800/month from employer = ₹3,600/month, or ₹43,200 per year. Even with 8.25% compounding, over 30 years this produces a corpus of approximately ₹55-60 lakh. In 2056 money, that is worth roughly ₹17-20 lakh today.

For a retirement that requires ₹50,000/month in today's terms — a modest, not extravagant, life — you need a corpus of roughly ₹1.5-2 crore in today's purchasing power. EPF, at mandatory contribution levels, delivers less than 15% of that.

The generous reading is that EPF was never designed to be your entire retirement. It was designed as a floor. The ungenerous reading is that the floor has a large hole in it, and most people do not notice because they have never looked down.

## The tax twist

EPF interest above ₹2.5 lakh per year (employee contribution) is now taxable. If your basic salary is ₹1 lakh per month, your annual EPF contribution is ₹1.44 lakh (employee) + ₹1.44 lakh (employer), generating interest well above the exemption limit. The post-tax return on the excess portion drops to roughly 6-6.5% — dangerously close to inflation and, in high-inflation years, below it.

## The alternative illusion

At this point, the financial advisor in the room will suggest equity mutual funds. They will show you a chart with 12-14% returns and a satisfied-looking couple. They will not show you the chart where the same couple's portfolio drops 40% in March 2020, or the chart where SIP returns over 3-5 years can be negative. EPF's virtue is not its return; it is its stability. You do not panic-sell EPF because you cannot. Illiquidity, in this one case, is a feature.

## The honest answer

EPF is a good product doing an inadequate job. It was designed in an era when joint families provided social security, when ₹5,000/month was a comfortable retirement, and when life expectancy was 58. None of these conditions persist. The product has not been redesigned; it has been incrementally adjusted, which is how you get a 1995 Maruti 800 with power windows — technically improved, fundamentally insufficient.

Your EPF should be the base of your retirement, not its extent. Use the calculator above to see where your current EPF trajectory lands you, then fill the gap with a combination of NPS (for the additional 80C deduction and equity exposure), PPF (for the tax-free compounding), and equity (for the real returns that actually beat inflation over 15+ year horizons).

8.25% is not nothing. It is also not enough. The space between those two statements is where your retirement planning should live.
