## The number that sells mutual funds

"NIFTY 50 has returned 12% CAGR since inception." Every mutual fund distributor has memorised this sentence. It is technically true and practically misleading in at least three ways.

## First problem: the average erases the journey

The NIFTY 50 was launched in 1995 at base 1,000. By March 2026 it sits around 23,000. That is roughly 12.4% CAGR over 31 years. Impressive.

Now consider what happened inside those 31 years. Between 2008 and 2013, the index went essentially nowhere — five years of stagnation. Between 1999 and 2002, it fell from 1,700 to below 1,000. If you started investing in 1999, your twelve-year return through 2011 was close to zero.

The average smooths over these dry spells. Your financial life does not. A child's education due in 2010 could not wait for the average to materialise.

## Second problem: survivorship bias

The NIFTY 50 of 2026 is not the NIFTY 50 of 1995. Between 1995 and 2025, SEBI data shows over 30 stocks were removed and replaced. Companies that failed, stagnated, or shrank were quietly substituted with ones that were growing. This is not fraud — it is how indices work. But it means the index's historical return is systematically higher than what any individual stock achieved, because losers were excised with the surgical precision of a dinner party host removing guests who embarrassed themselves.

If you had bought all 50 stocks in 1995 and held them without rebalancing, your returns would be meaningfully lower than 12%.

## Third problem: inflation and taxes eat the margin

Twelve percent nominal minus roughly 6% inflation leaves about 5.7% real. After LTCG tax at 12.5% on gains above the ₹1.25 lakh exemption, you are closer to 4.5% real. This is still a good return — far better than fixed deposits — but it is not the "12% compounded" that marketing pamphlets promise.

## What you should actually calculate

Instead of quoting CAGR, look at rolling returns. The NIFTY 50's 10-year rolling return has varied from roughly 5% to 18% depending on when you measured. Your entry point determines your outcome far more than the long-run average suggests.

## The honest framing

NIFTY's 12% is not a lie. It is a summary statistic that conceals the variance, the selection bias, and the purchasing power erosion underneath. Treat it as a ceiling for expectations, not a floor. Plan for 8-10% nominal and 4-5% real, and you will rarely be disappointed.
