## The party that missed the point

When the BSE Sensex crossed 80,000 in late 2025, business channels ran special segments. Anchors beamed. Experts called it a "new era." WhatsApp groups forwarded celebratory messages from people who own exactly zero equities.

Let us apply some arithmetic to the celebration.

## What 80,000 actually means in 2005 money

The Sensex was at roughly 8,000 in early 2005. It is at roughly 80,000 now. That is a 10x increase in 21 years, or about 11.6% CAGR. Decent.

Now adjust for inflation. CPI inflation averaged roughly 6.5% annually over this period. ₹1 in 2005 is worth about ₹3.70 today. So the Sensex's 10x nominal increase is roughly a 2.7x increase in real terms — about 4.9% real CAGR.

A 4.9% real return over two decades is respectable. It is not a cause for euphoria. It is the sort of return that builds wealth slowly, which is what equities are supposed to do.

## The rupee depreciation factor

There is a second erosion most people ignore. The Indian rupee was at roughly ₹43.5 per USD in 2005. By April 2026, it is around ₹84 per USD. That is roughly 50% depreciation. If you measure the Sensex in USD, the 10x becomes roughly a 5.2x increase — about 8.2% CAGR in dollar terms. Not bad, but a different story.

This matters if you plan to spend money abroad — children's education, travel, imported goods. The Sensex's headline number does not account for the fact that the rupee it is denominated in buys far fewer dollars than it used to.

## What your SIP actually earned

A ₹10,000 monthly SIP in a Sensex-tracking fund from 2005 to 2026 would have accumulated roughly ₹1.05 crore nominally. After 6.5% average inflation, that is about ₹28.4 lakh in 2005 purchasing power. After LTCG tax, closer to ₹25.5 lakh real.

Is this good? Yes. Is it "Sensex at 80,000" good? Only if you ignore the denominator.

## Why milestones are a marketing device

Index milestones are psychologically powerful but financially meaningless. The Sensex at 80,000 is not structurally different from the Sensex at 79,500. It simply crossed a round number in a counting system humans invented because we have ten fingers.

The meaningful question is not "what is the Sensex at?" but "what is my real return after costs, taxes, and inflation?" That number is rarely cause for celebration. It is usually cause for quiet, patient continuation — which is what investing actually requires.

## The bottom line

You are richer than you were in 2005, but not 10x richer. You are roughly 2.5-3x richer in real purchasing power if you held equities the entire time. Celebrate moderately. Continue investing. Ignore the round numbers.
