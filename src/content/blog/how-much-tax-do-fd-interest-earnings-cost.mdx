## The comfort of certainty

Fixed deposits are India's default investment. Your parents recommend them. Your bank's homepage promotes them. The interest rate is printed on the receipt. Everything feels safe and knowable.

But the number on that receipt is not what you keep.

## The tax on FD interest

FD interest is fully taxable as "Income from Other Sources." It is added to your total income and taxed at your slab rate. There is no special rate, no indexation, no holding period benefit.

At the 30% slab (plus 4% cess = 31.2% effective), a 7.1% FD yields:

- Gross return: 7.1%
- Tax on interest: 7.1% × 31.2% = 2.22%
- Net return: **4.88%**

That's before inflation.

## The inflation eraser

CPI inflation in India has averaged roughly 5–6% in recent years. Let's use 5.5% as a reasonable estimate.

- Net return after tax: 4.88%
- Inflation: 5.5%
- **Real return: -0.62%**

You are losing money. Not dramatically, not catastrophically, but persistently. Every year, your FD's purchasing power declines by roughly 0.6%. Over 10 years, that compounds to a 6% loss in real terms.

## The TDS trap

Banks deduct 10% TDS on FD interest above ₹40,000 per year (₹50,000 for senior citizens). If your slab rate is 30%, you owe the remaining 21.2% as self-assessment tax when you file returns. Many people either forget this or discover it as a surprise tax bill in July.

If you're in the 10% or 20% slab, the TDS may be close to your actual liability. But if you submit Form 15G/15H (declaring that your total income is below the taxable limit), the bank won't deduct TDS at all. Many eligible people don't know this.

## The cumulative vs non-cumulative choice

Cumulative FDs reinvest interest annually — you pay tax each year on interest you haven't received. Non-cumulative FDs pay interest quarterly/monthly — you receive the cash and pay the tax in the same year. For taxpayers in higher slabs, cumulative FDs create a cash flow problem: tax outflow without interest income to fund it.

## What other options look like

Debt mutual funds, after the 2023 tax change, are also taxed at slab rate for gains held under 3 years — so they're no longer the tax arbitrage they once were. But they still offer better post-tax returns than FDs for the same duration, because their yields are marginally higher and expenses are lower.

## The bottom line

FDs are not investments. They are capital preservation tools with a negative real return for most taxpayers. Use them for emergency funds and short-term parking. For anything beyond 3 years, the post-tax, post-inflation math makes them objectively inferior to several alternatives. The comfort of the printed interest rate is expensive.
